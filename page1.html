Introduction

Despite being rich in natural resources and one of the biggest economies in the world, it has high income disparity, lack of job security, and high public debt. In lower income regions, they struggle with clean water and sanitation, underfunded education, and fewer healthcare facilities. We want to spread awareness of the inequality many Brazillians face against these issues and more.

This issue is closely connected to the United Nations Sustainable Development Goal Target 1.4 that emphasizes that all people should have equal rights to economic resources and access to basic services such as healthcare, education, and financial systems. Many low-income communities still struggle to access these essentials, making it harder to find stable employment, improve living conditions, or achieve longterm financial security.

Key Statistics

  • Brazil's Gini coefficient is around 0.52, meaning more concentrated wealth
  • Around 48.9 million people live below the poverty line
  • 20.4% of informal workers are poor compared to 6.7% of formal workers who are poor
  • Only 23% of Brazilians aged 23-34 have completed higher education

What Social Inequality Looks Like

Social inequality in Brazil can be seen in everyday life through differences in income, education, and access to basic services. Wealthier individuals often have access to private healthcare, high-quality education, and stable employment, while lower-income populations rely on underfunded public services.

Many people face challenges such as overcrowded housing, limited transportation, and fewer job opportunities. Informal work is also common, meaning many workers do not have stable income, benefits, or access to financial systems like banking and credit.

Hunger from Inequality

Hunger in Brazil | ARTE.tv Documentary

Hunger is one of the most serious consequences of social inequality in Brazil. Even though Brazil is a major global food producer, millions of people still face food insecurity due to low income, unemployment, and uneven access to resources. Many families in low-income communities struggle to afford enough nutritious food on a daily basis.

Hunger is often linked to unstable jobs and informal work, where wages are not reliable and workers lack financial security. Rising food prices and economic instability also make it harder for poor households to maintain a healthy diet.

Solutions to Social Inequality

Solving inequality in Brazil requires action in education, jobs, social support, and government accountability.

Education

Investing in public schools, teacher training, and learning resources helps break the cycle of poverty and gives young people better opportunities for the future.

Employment

Creating formal job opportunities and improving worker protections reduces reliance on informal, unstable work and increases financial security.

Social Programs

Income support, food assistance, and housing programs help families meet basic needs while working toward long-term stability.

Government Accountability

Reducing corruption and improving transparency ensures that public resources are used effectively to support low-income communities.
